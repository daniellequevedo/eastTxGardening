Things I've learned as an East Texas Vegetable Gardener

The Benefits

There a several benefits to taking up vegetable gardening. It can be fun, relaxing, and therapeutic. It can also be very convenient to bring in vegetables from the backyard as opposed to the grocery store. And lastly, it's healthy; it's easier to eat vegetables when there's so many available from your garden.

The Tools

Every gardener needs a reliable set of tools:

  • A Spade Shovel
    If you're not using raised beds filled with bagged dirt and your garden will be planted directly in the ground, then you'll need a spade shovel for breaking ground when you first start your garden each season.
  • A Hoe
    You'll also need a sturdy hoe of good quality for maintaining your garden.
  • A Garden Trowel
    If your garden is small and/or easy to reach (less than 100 square feet or you have raised beds that are higher than your knees), then a garden trowel will work fine in place of a spade and hoe. The spade and hoe are more for big jobs.
  • A Small Paring Knife
    This is good for thinning out crowded seedlings, no matter your garden's size.

And remember to always keep your tools clean and sharp!

Gardening Basics

There are a few basic lessons I've learned since I first started vegetable gardening:

Water

Make sure you have a way to easily water your garden, either through a sprinkler system or drip irrigation with soaker hoses. Check your garden daily to make sure it's getting enough water. At the same time, don't over-water.

Fertilize

Always fertilize a week or so before you plant. And it's best to use an organic fertlizer. Gardening When it Counts by Steve Solomon teaches you all about organic fertilizer in the first chapter.

Plan Ahead!

If you're planting tomato plants, put the tomato cage around around them while they're still small. If you're planting climbing peas or climbing beans, install the wall of plastic mesh or chicken wire first, then sow the seeds right under the mesh / wire. It's also good to think about what plants grow best when planted near each other (Companion Planting), and to consider last year's garden plan when creating the one for this year (Succession Planting). Also pick a spot that'll be sheltered from strong winds, but will also have full sun from at least 10am to 4pm.

To Everything There Is A Season

Don't go planting Okra in November. You'll just be wasting seeds. Each vegetable has one or two specific windows a year in which it's best to be planted. Plants that are sown outside of their window simply won't flourish (unless of course you have a fancy climate-controlled greenhouse). The Texas A&M East Texas Gardening website has a list of planting dates for a variety of vegetables.

Whenever Possible, Plant From Seed

It's tempting to bring home small starter plants from Wal-Mart or HomeDepot and transplant them in your garden. But chances are they'll never grow as big and strong and ones you planted straight from seed. Remember, patience is a virtue!

Deeper Topics

If you really want to be an expert gardener, you'll want to explore these topics as well:

Companion Planting

Some plants do well when planted together. Some are best kept apart:

Grow These Together:

  • Icicle Radishes help keep squash bugs off of Squash plants.
  • Pea plants fix the level of nitrogen in the soil, making it perfect for Carrots.
  • Okra protects the more fragile Bell Pepper plant from strong winds.

Keep These Apart:

  • Potatoes have a greater chance of developing phytophthora blight when grown near cucumbers.
  • The growth of cabbage, broccoli, and brussel sprouts are inhibted by strawberries.
  • The growth of beans is inhibited by onions, garlic, shallots, and chives.

Succession Planting (Crop Rotation)

Always keep a record of what you planted and where you planted it. Different vegetables pull different nutrients out of the soil, and they also replace different nutrients in the soil. So it's best to plant everything in a particular order. Growveg.com is a good website to check out for Succession Planting.

Organic vs. Non-Organic

Organic vegetables means they were grown without pesticides and chemical fertilizers. If you're buying seeds, look for organic seeds. That means they came from plants that were grown organically.

Hybrid vs. Non-Hybrid

All store-bought vegetables and store-bought seed packets today are Hybrids, meaning they're a mix of more than one plant variety—Several varieties may have been cross-bred to result in bigger, hardier plants. Often, this involves genetic modification, including "terminator genes" that prevent you from being able to re-use seeds from the same set of plants year after year. With Non-Hyrid seeds (also known as open-pollenated or heirloom seeds), on the other hand, you can save the seeds and replant every time, and you'll get a consistent variety year after year. These are no different than the seeds used a hundred years ago. The only way to get Non-Hybrid seeds is by purchasing them online. Baker Creek is a good website for Non-Hybrid, non-Genetically Modified seeds.
